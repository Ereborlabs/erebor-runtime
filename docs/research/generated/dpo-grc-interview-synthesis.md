## Problem Statement

Erebor is trying to become the trust boundary for AI agents that operate real systems: browsing, running commands, using tools, touching SaaS access, and acting near customer or personal data.

After Amy, Alex, George, Kayne, and Tony, the problem is more validated than before:

- legal/privacy/GRC/IT reviewers understand the risk of agents going off-script
- they ask for mandate, guardrails, audit trail, stop/revoke, and failure-response evidence
- DPO/legal are usually not the economic buyer, but their concern can delay deployment or force the provider/team to produce stronger evidence
- IT, compliance, security, AI platform, and agent vendors are closer to budget and deployment

The competitor landscape now matters. Salus and Zenity show this category is real, but they also force Erebor to be precise.

If Erebor says only "we validate actions before execution," Salus is already too close.

If Erebor says only "we secure agents everywhere," Zenity is already the broad enterprise platform.

Updated claim check:

- Salus is clearly on the action/tool path: validate the agent's proposed action before it executes.
- Zenity is broader than tool-call governance. It covers observability, posture management, detection, response, inline controls, and platform-level enterprise security workflows.
- The better bypass claim is not "both are only tool-call governance." The better claim is: any governance layer can be bypassed if the agent has unmanaged action paths outside that layer, such as browser, desktop, process, shell, raw API, or SaaS UI paths that do not pass through the governed integration.

The differentiated claim should be:

> Erebor creates a governed execution session around an agent operating real computer/tool surfaces, enforces the approved mandate outside the agent, and produces the evidence packet that IT, compliance, privacy, security, or an enterprise buyer can review.

## Demand Evidence

### What The Interviews Validate

Amy:

- Review starts with the agent mandate, not the control mechanism.
- The reviewer needs intended use, operating boundaries, data categories, approval/supervision authority, and responsibility allocation.
- Governance is a product feature if it helps enterprise customers trust the system.

Alex:

- Generic "task completed" logs are useless.
- Reviewers need step-by-step evidence, blocked attempts, control during sensitive or irreversible actions, and logs outside the agent's control.
- No kill switch means no pilot approval.

George:

- The strongest phrase is "actual blockade."
- Some DPO/legal teams can escalate, force DPIA review, stop workflows, or own budget in larger organizations.
- His Who / Where / Why frame is the simplest reviewer packet: who is behind the system, where data is processed, why the agent needs that data or authority.
- Runtime behavior matters because a vendor agent was caught reading passwords during testing.

Kayne:

- GRC/legal/privacy may not be buyer or guaranteed blocker.
- They may document risk, shift responsibility through contracts, or avoid being the team that says no.
- The stronger pressure may land on agent vendors or internal deployment owners who need enterprise trust.

Tony:

- Tony initially confused Erebor with a governance agent. That is a messaging warning.
- Once clarified, he understood Erebor as a sandbox/runtime around agents.
- His first concern was agents going rogue, ignoring instructions, or doing harmful things.
- His buying path points to IT and compliance, not privacy/legal alone.
- His minimum evidence is an audit trail that explains what happened and supports reversal or containment.
- Conservative companies that want AI but worry about control are more promising than companies already all-in on AI.

### What Is Still Not Validated

- No buyer has said "I will pay for Erebor."
- No agent vendor has said a deal was delayed because they lacked this exact control/evidence layer.
- No IT/compliance/security owner has committed to a design pilot.
- No DPO/GRC interview has yet produced a named deployment owner with a blocked workflow.

Verdict: the problem is validated. The buyer is not yet validated. The next proof must be a buyer-path proof, not another "reviewers agree this matters" proof.

## External Landscape

### Salus

Public positioning:

- YC W26.
- "Guardrails to validate your agent's actions before they execute."
- API/runtime that wraps around an agent, checks actions at runtime, blocks incorrect ones, and gives feedback for retries.
- Website says it sits between every agent and every tool, deciding which actions ship, get clarified, repaired, or held.
- It is a policy-aware proxy in front of tools. One endpoint change puts it on the hot path.
- It supports OpenAI, Anthropic, Gemini, LangChain, LangGraph, CrewAI, LlamaIndex, Retell, Vapi, MCP servers, REST, SDKs, and internal services.

Source notes:

- YC profile: https://www.ycombinator.com/companies/salus
- Website: https://www.usesalus.ai/

Interpretation:

Salus is the closest direct competitor on action-time validation. They have very good positioning: "commit-time is the moment of judgment." If Erebor competes only as "runtime guardrails before execution," Salus owns that sentence.

Salus also appears to assume a cooperative integration: the agent builder wraps the agent or tool path with the Salus API, and Salus checks the proposed action at runtime, blocks incorrect actions, and returns feedback so the agent can retry. That is valuable, but it is different from a containment boundary around a semi-trusted or compromised agent.

The bypass critique is coverage, not quality. If every meaningful action goes through a Salus-governed tool gateway, Salus is strong. If the agent can act through a browser, process, desktop automation, shell, raw API client, or SaaS UI path that does not pass through that gateway, then the governance layer can be routed around.

Where Salus looks strong:

- agent correctness and business-rule validation
- low-friction developer integration
- cooperative agent/tool integration where the builder wants the guardrail
- tool-call proxy path
- action repair/retry
- shadow replay and policy promotion

Where Erebor can differ:

- governed session rather than single tool proxy
- computer/browser/process/runtime boundary, not only declared tool calls
- explicit proof of which action surfaces are controlled, observed, blocked, or outside scope
- useful even when the agent is wrong, compromised, confused, or trying an alternate route
- evidence packet for privacy, compliance, IT, security, or enterprise review
- self-hosted/customer-controlled deployment for regulated buyers
- residual-risk language and reviewer signoff, not only action feedback to the agent
- stop/revoke/failure-response story for pilots

### Zenity

Public positioning:

- "Secure AI Agents Everywhere."
- Unified observability, governance, and threat protection for agents on any platform.
- Capabilities include AI Observability, AI Security Posture Management, and AI Detection and Response.
- Zenity says security teams lack visibility into which agents exist, who built them, what they can access, and runtime behavior.
- AIDR monitors step-level agent execution, correlates behavior with context, and enforces inline controls to stop unsafe actions.
- AIDR also claims full execution graph mapping, memory/context tracking, prompt-injection blocking, data exfiltration blocking, privilege escalation monitoring, permission revocation, execution blocking, and automated remediation.

Source notes:

- Home page: https://zenity.io/
- AIDR page: https://zenity.io/platform/ai-security-platform/aidr

Interpretation:

Zenity validates the enterprise category. Security teams need AI-agent observability, posture management, detection, and response. But Zenity is broad and top-down. It is trying to be the platform for enterprise security teams across SaaS, device-based, and homegrown agents.

Do not reduce Zenity to "tool-call governance." Zenity's stronger claim is broader: inventory, runtime behavior, execution graph visibility, inline controls, and security response. Erebor should not pretend Zenity has no runtime controls. Erebor should instead argue that a narrow governed session can give a DPO, GRC reviewer, IT owner, or agent vendor a concrete proof packet for one risky workflow faster than a broad enterprise rollout.

Where Zenity looks strong:

- enterprise security buyer
- inventory and visibility across agent ecosystems
- posture management
- detection and response
- incident response workflows
- broad platform coverage

Where Erebor can differ:

- narrower governed-runtime wedge for one high-risk agent workflow
- developer/operator primitive that can ship before a full enterprise platform sale
- DPO/IT/compliance evidence packet generated from a real governed run
- session-level boundary proof: what the agent could touch, what it did touch, what was blocked, and what remained outside control
- self-hosted design-partner package for regulated pilots
- focus on allowing a blocked or risky workflow to proceed under constraints, not only enterprise-wide security monitoring
- "agent provider selling into enterprise" wedge, not only buyer-side security program

## Premises

1. Erebor is valid only if it becomes the thing that helps a real agent workflow get approved, sold, or deployed sooner.
2. DPO/GRC interviews are useful if they reveal the approval bar and the buyer path, not if they only produce abstract agreement.
3. Salus is the closest competitor on pre-execution action validation for cooperative agents. Erebor must not sound like a weaker Salus, and must prove that browser/process/desktop/raw-API actions cannot route around the governed path.
4. Zenity is the broad enterprise security-platform competitor. Erebor must not misrepresent Zenity as merely tool-call governance or try to out-Zenity Zenity from a pre-product position.
5. Erebor's differentiated wedge is a governed session around real computer/tool authority plus a reviewer-ready evidence packet, with explicit residual-risk language for any surface it does not control.

## Approaches Considered

### Approach A: Compete With Salus On Runtime Action Validation

Summary: Position Erebor as a runtime guardrail that validates agent actions before execution.

Effort: M
Risk: High

Pros:

- Simple market language.
- Directly matches the "agent goes rogue" concern from Tony.
- Easy for agent vendors to understand.

Cons:

- Salus already has this framing and a clean integration story.
- Risks reducing Erebor to a proxy around tool calls.
- Does not use the DPO/GRC interview insight around mandate, review packet, role allocation, and residual risk.

Reuses: policy checks, action blocking, audit trail.

### Approach B: Compete With Zenity On Enterprise AI-Agent Security

Summary: Position Erebor as an enterprise platform for AI-agent observability, governance, detection, and response.

Effort: XL
Risk: Very High

Pros:

- Validated enterprise category.
- Security teams already understand visibility, posture, detection, and response.
- Big platform opportunity if it works.

Cons:

- Zenity is already broad, polished, and enterprise-oriented.
- Too much product surface before buyer proof.
- Pulls Erebor away from the sharp governed-runtime proof it can actually show.

Reuses: audit model, future UI/reporting, policy enforcement.

### Approach C: Own The Governed Session Evidence Wedge

Summary: Position Erebor as the governed execution session for agents operating real systems. The session starts with an approved mandate, mediates browser/process/tool/API surfaces outside the agent, blocks or holds risky actions, marks unmanaged escape paths as residual risk, and exports an evidence packet for IT/compliance/security/privacy review.

Effort: M/L
Risk: Medium

Pros:

- Uses the strongest interview signal: mandate + blockade + trace + stop/revoke.
- Differentiates from Salus by going beyond tool-call validation into session evidence and controlled surfaces.
- Differentiates from Zenity by being a narrow deployable wedge, not an enterprise platform replacement.
- Fits agent vendors selling into conservative or regulated buyers.
- Creates a concrete demo artifact: one governed run, one blocked action, one evidence packet.
- Turns the bypass claim into a product requirement: either mediate the surface or name it as not controlled.

Cons:

- Messaging must be disciplined or it collapses back into Salus/Zenity territory.
- Requires enough runtime proof to be credible, especially browser/process/tool action provenance.
- If an agent can step outside Erebor's governed path, Erebor must call the mode "observed" or "cooperative," not enforced.
- Buyer still needs validation with IT/compliance/security or agent providers.

Reuses: governed OpenClaw pilot, OAuth authority-transition demo, process interception, JSONL audit, DPO evidence trace, Tony's "not an agent" clarification.

## Recommended Approach

Choose Approach C.

Do not pitch Erebor as:

- "runtime guardrails for every agent action" - too close to Salus
- "AI-agent security platform" - too close to Zenity
- "DPO approval automation" - wrong buyer and legally sloppy

Pitch it as:

> Erebor is the governed execution session for AI agents operating real systems. It defines what the agent is allowed to do, enforces that boundary outside the agent, blocks or holds risky actions, and produces the evidence packet IT, compliance, security, or privacy reviewers need to approve a limited pilot.

Shorter version:

> Erebor is not the agent. It is the control boundary and evidence trace around the agent.

Critical caveat:

> Erebor only wins if the agent cannot route around the governed path, or if the evidence packet plainly states which surfaces were not controlled.

Sharper vendor version:

> If your agent vendor deal slows down because IT, security, or privacy asks "what can this agent actually do?", Erebor gives you a governed run and the proof packet to answer.

## The Next DPO/GRC Interview

The remaining interview should not ask "is Erebor useful?" It should test whether the reviewer would distinguish Erebor from Salus-like action validation, Zenity-like enterprise monitoring, and a runtime that actually controls the action surfaces an agent can use.

Ask:

```text
If a vendor told you they validate every tool action before execution, would that be enough for review?
```

Then:

```text
What would still be missing: mandate, deployment model, data location, stop/revoke, audit trail, blocked attempts, unmanaged browser/process/API paths, or proof from a real run?
```

Ask the bypass version directly:

```text
If the agent can act through a browser, shell, desktop automation, SaaS UI, or raw API path outside the tool gateway, would tool-action validation still be enough?
```

Then:

```text
Would you need proof that the runtime owns those action surfaces, or is after-the-fact audit acceptable?
```

Ask:

```text
If your security team already had a broad AI-agent monitoring platform, would that be enough to approve a limited pilot?
```

Then:

```text
What evidence would you still want from the actual run before approving the workflow?
```

Force the buying path:

```text
When you raise a concern, who has to fix it: the vendor, IT, compliance, security, AI platform, or the business owner?
```

The best possible answer:

> "A tool-action guardrail helps, and a security platform helps, but for approval I need the specific workflow mandate, what action surfaces the agent could use, what it touched, what was blocked, who can stop it, and what residual risk remains."

That answer validates Erebor's wedge.

## Success Criteria

Erebor gets stronger if the next 10 buyer-path interviews produce:

- 3 agent vendors or deployment owners who have seen enterprise review friction around agent autonomy
- 2 who say runtime action validation alone is not enough because reviewers need session-level evidence and coverage of unmanaged action paths
- 2 who ask whether browser/process/desktop/raw-API paths are controlled, not just whether declared tool calls are checked
- 1 who says a governed run packet would help a real deal, pilot, or internal approval
- 1 design partner willing to compare an Erebor evidence trace against their review process

Erebor gets weaker if:

- buyers say Salus-like action validation is enough
- security teams say Zenity-like platform coverage is enough
- reviewers do not care whether the agent can bypass a tool gateway through browser/process/API surfaces
- nobody can name a workflow that is delayed, narrowed, or blocked
- reviewers keep saying "interesting" but cannot identify the owner who must fix the gap

## The Assignment

For the next interview, test the differentiator directly:

1. Explain Erebor in one sentence: "Erebor is not an agent; it is the governed runtime and evidence trace around an agent."
2. Ask whether action validation before execution would be enough.
3. Ask whether that answer changes if the agent can act through browser, shell, desktop, SaaS UI, or raw API paths outside the tool gateway.
4. Ask whether broad enterprise AI security monitoring would be enough.
5. Ask what specific evidence from a real run would still be required.
6. Ask who has to fix the gap when review raises a concern.

After the call, classify the signal:

- Strong Erebor signal: needs governed session evidence beyond action validation and broad monitoring.
- Stronger Erebor signal: explicitly asks for proof that unmanaged action paths are controlled or marked as residual risk.
- Salus-compression risk: action validation is enough.
- Zenity-compression risk: enterprise platform monitoring is enough.
- Weak signal: no real workflow, no owner, no consequence.
