## Problem Statement

The founder has two working MVPs:

- Erebor: a governed runtime for AI agent sessions, currently aimed at an evidence-trace demo where an agent acts, Erebor blocks risky authority transitions, and the run becomes reviewable by DPO, security, GRC, or AI-platform stakeholders.
- MergeDB: a Git-like database branching substrate for AI agents, currently aimed at enterprise DB platform teams that need agents to work against realistic Oracle, SQL Server, DB2, or similar data without giving agents unsafe access to source systems.

Both are plausible startup wedges. The founder has lived the Erebor pain personally and has worked in the MergeDB industry. That means this should not be framed as one idea having founder-market fit and the other lacking it.

The sharper decision is pursuit order: continue Erebor as the primary startup wedge now, while preserving MergeDB as a later governed-data surface and validating it lightly through existing database contacts.

MergeDB may have the bigger infrastructure prize, but it needs the MergeDB promotion trigger before it should become the primary bet: a real DB platform lead names a workflow, source system, schema or approved fixture, acceptance criteria, and willingness to pilot.

## Status Quo

Erebor status quo:

- Teams block agents near sensitive workflows, or keep them in toy/synthetic environments.
- Security, privacy, GRC, and AI-platform teams lack defensible evidence about what an agent touched, attempted, and was blocked from doing.
- Current review often becomes policy documents, screenshots, approvals, and trust in the agent rather than enforced runtime evidence.

MergeDB status quo:

- Teams use staging databases, masked dumps, seed data, fixtures, or manual data workflows.
- These environments drift from production and do not let agents safely test realistic reads and writes.
- DB platform teams are unlikely to give agents broad write access to Oracle, SQL Server, DB2, or other enterprise systems without isolation, masking, temporary credentials, diffing, and cleanup.

Both status quos are credible. Neither has been validated with named buyer behavior yet.

## Target User & Narrowest Wedge

Primary wedge now: Erebor evidence trace.

Target:

- Primary persona for the next cycle: a DPO or privacy lead at an EU-facing B2B SaaS or regulated enterprise.
- Secondary learning targets: GRC, security engineering, and AI-platform leads who may own deployment or budget.
- The primary persona needs to approve or block agent workflows that may touch sensitive systems, personal data, or authority transitions.
- They get credit for enabling safe AI adoption with evidence.
- They get blamed when an agent touches data or systems without a defensible record.

Narrowest wedge:

- One governed agent workflow.
- One clear risky authority transition.
- One pre-action block or hold.
- One rendered evidence trace showing actor, purpose, governed resources, allowed actions, denied actions, policy version, residual risk, and non-claims.

Working definitions:

- Evidence trace: a reviewable packet from a governed run: actor, purpose, controlled surfaces, governed resources exposed, allowed actions, blocked or held actions, policy version, audit hash, and residual risk.
- Controlled surface: a browser, shell, tool, API, filesystem path, or future data branch whose effects pass through Erebor before execution.
- Residual risk: what the trace does not prove, for example host-wide containment, semantic PII classification, or legal compliance.
- Non-claim: an explicit statement that Erebor is not guaranteeing legal compliance or proving the agent never saw PII outside controlled surfaces.

Minimum trace example:

| Field | Example |
| --- | --- |
| Actor | `openclaw` agent |
| Purpose | "Investigate OAuth support thread without granting new authority" |
| Controlled surfaces | Governed browser CDP, bounded process guard |
| Exposed resources | OAuth lab URL, GitHub OAuth authorize page |
| Blocked action | OAuth callback handoff or unmanaged browser launch |
| Policy version | `policy.json` plus SHA-256 hash |
| Residual risk | No full-device containment claim; no semantic PII classifier |

Secondary future surface: MergeDB.

MergeDB should be validated as a future Erebor-governed data surface:

- Erebor governs the agent session and action boundary.
- MergeDB provides isolated read/write data branches as one controlled surface inside that session.
- The first MergeDB validation remains independent: find one DB platform lead with one real workflow where staging/seed data blocks agent usefulness.

Customer-facing positioning should stay Erebor-only for now. "MergeDB as a future governed data surface" is internal strategy until a buyer validates that data branches are a must-have controlled surface.

## Constraints

- Do not combine the products into one broad platform before demand exists.
- Do not abandon MergeDB; preserve it as an option and keep learning from database interviews.
- Do not position Erebor as legal compliance automation. The honest claim is governed-session evidence and enforcement for controlled surfaces.
- Do not position MergeDB as a generic Postgres clone. Its differentiated opening is enterprise data systems and branch lifecycle for agents.
- Do not build more before the next interviews unless the build directly improves the conversation artifact.
- The next decision must be based on buyer pull, not founder taste alone.

## Premises

1. Both ideas have founder-market fit: Erebor through lived pain, MergeDB through domain background.
2. With no users yet, the right decision is not permanent company strategy; it is which wedge deserves primary focus for the next validation cycle.
3. Erebor should lead now because its evidence trace can be reviewed without integrating into a buyer's real systems.
4. MergeDB should remain active as a lightweight validation track and future Erebor surface.
5. MergeDB can overtake Erebor if it hits the promotion trigger: workflow, source system, schema or approved fixture, acceptance criteria, and willingness to pilot.
6. The best long-term architecture may be Erebor controlling multiple agent surfaces, with MergeDB as the governed data-branch surface, but that is a later expansion.

Database branching for agents is also real. Neon and Ardent show market language around branches/copies for AI workflows, while BranchBench argues current branchable DB systems still leave gaps for agentic workloads:

- BranchBench: https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.17180
- Neon branching docs: https://neon.com/docs/introduction/branching
- Ardent: https://www.tryardent.com/

Eureka: the two ideas do not need to compete forever. Erebor can be the session-level governance company, and MergeDB can later become one high-value controlled data surface inside governed sessions. The mistake would be trying to sell that whole architecture before either wedge has external pull.

## Approaches Considered

### Approach A: Head-to-Head Validation Race

Run both tracks equally for 7-10 days, using the same scorecard and no major build work.

Effort: S
Risk: Low

Pros:

- Avoids premature founder bias.
- Uses the meetings and contacts already available.
- Produces a real decision from buyer reactions.

Cons:

- Delays focus.
- Can become fake rigor if calls are not scored hard.
- Does not exploit Erebor's more mature buyer-review artifact.

### Approach B: Erebor First

Put most energy into Erebor's DPO/security evidence-trace wedge and keep MergeDB as a later or side project.

Effort: M
Risk: Medium

Pros:

- Fastest path to a buyer-reviewable artifact.
- Strong current market urgency around agent governance.
- Repo already has a polished OpenClaw/OAuth/evidence runbook.

Cons:

- Crowded category.
- DPO may be stakeholder, not buyer.
- Could lose the MergeDB domain advantage if ignored too long.

### Approach C: Full Unified Governed Agent Infrastructure

Build and pitch the combined platform now: Erebor governs action surfaces, MergeDB governs data branches, and the product becomes one agent infrastructure stack.

Effort: XL
Risk: High

Pros:

- Biggest long-term thesis.
- Coherent architecture if both wedges work.
- Stronger defensibility if the system owns multiple controlled surfaces.

Cons:

- Too broad before demand.
- Hard to explain in one buyer call.
- Splits focus exactly when customer truth matters most.

### Approach D: Erebor Now, MergeDB Later As Surface

Continue Erebor as the primary wedge, but explicitly keep MergeDB alive as a future governed-data surface. Run lightweight MergeDB interviews and only promote MergeDB if it hits the promotion trigger.

Effort: S/M
Risk: Low/Medium

Pros:

- Keeps one primary story for the market.
- Preserves the MergeDB insight instead of throwing it away.
- Turns the combined architecture into an expansion path, not a starting burden.

Cons:

- MergeDB may feel slower emotionally because it is not the headline bet this week.
- Requires discipline to avoid building the MergeDB surface prematurely.
- If MergeDB calls are unusually strong, the plan must be willing to flip.

## Recommended Approach

Choose Approach D: Erebor now, MergeDB later as surface.

This is the cleanest answer to "which idea is better for pursuing?" Pursue Erebor as the primary company wedge now. Do not kill MergeDB. Treat MergeDB as a future surface candidate inside Erebor's governed-session thesis, not as customer-facing positioning yet.

The customer-facing positioning becomes:

> Erebor is the governed runtime for AI agents. It controls risky actions, records what happened, and produces evidence a security/privacy owner can review.

The internal expansion thesis is:

> Over time, Erebor can govern more surfaces: browser, shell, tools, APIs, filesystem, and data branches. MergeDB is the candidate data-branch surface for enterprise data-heavy agents.

This gives the founder focus without destroying optionality.

## Success Criteria

Erebor wins the next cycle if:

- At least two target stakeholders give an observable pull signal: sharing an internal checklist, asking for a follow-up with a deployer, requesting a pilot review, or introducing a budget owner.
- At least one person agrees to compare the trace against their internal AI/security/privacy review checklist.
- A budget path is identified: named person, department, procurement route, or budget category. "Security might pay" is not enough.
- The demo can show action, block/hold, evidence trace, and residual risk in under 10 minutes.
- Before interviews, run one dry rehearsal and confirm the artifact can be shown in under 10 minutes without hand-waving.

MergeDB gets promoted if:

- One DB platform or data-platform lead names a real blocked workflow.
- They name the source system: Oracle, SQL Server, DB2, Postgres, MySQL, or equivalent.
- They provide a representative schema or agree to a buyer-approved fixture.
- They define pilot acceptance criteria.
- They say an isolated read/write branch with temporary credentials, masking, diff, and cleanup would be worth piloting.

Kill or pause criteria:

- Pause Erebor-first if interviews say the evidence trace is nice-to-have and the real blocker is endpoint deployment, identity, or budget ownership outside the current wedge.
- Pause MergeDB if interviews only validate developer convenience on Postgres-like systems and do not reveal enterprise DB platform urgency.

## Validation Scorecard

Qualified conversation definition: the respondent matches a target persona, owns or participates in the current workflow, can describe the status quo cost, and can name a plausible next-step owner.

Score each track from 0-3 after every qualified conversation.

| Factor | 0 | 1 | 2 | 3 |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Urgency | Abstract interest | Pain exists someday | Current active blocker | Urgent enough to change roadmap |
| Status quo cost | Unknown | Annoying workaround | Clear time/risk/cost | Named incident, delay, or blocked rollout |
| Buyer path | Unknown | Evaluator only | Deployer or budget owner named | Budget owner agrees to next step |
| Pilot willingness | Polite feedback | Wants updates | Asks for artifact review | Commits to pilot/checklist review |
| Integration friction | Impossible/unclear | Heavy custom setup | Feasible with fixture | Feasible with current MVP/demo |
| Competitive pressure | Crowded and undifferentiated | Some differentiation | Clear wedge vs alternatives | Buyer says current tools do not solve it |
| Founder access | Cold/no access | Weak intro | Warm intro | Meeting with target stakeholder |

Decision rule:

- Compare average score per qualified call, not raw total score, because the two tracks may get different call counts.
- Continue Erebor primary unless MergeDB beats Erebor by at least 0.75 average points per call or hits the MergeDB promotion trigger.
- If both average under 1.5 after qualified calls, extend discovery instead of building.
- If fewer than three qualified calls happen in 7 days, do not make a final decision. Extend the validation window by one week.
