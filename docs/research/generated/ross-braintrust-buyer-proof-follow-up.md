## Status Quo

The status quo to attack is agents stuck in toy pilots and manual review.

The current workaround is a mix of:

- keep agents away from real authority
- run them in test data, sandboxes, or narrow pilots
- rely on human review and approval
- ask vendors for questionnaires, contracts, and security assurances
- inspect logs or traces after the fact
- rely on observability and eval tools to understand behavior
- accept residual risk and deploy anyway

That is only painful if it blocks something real:

- a workflow cannot move from toy data into production-like use
- IT cannot approve an integration
- risk/compliance cannot accept the authority boundary
- a vendor cannot answer enterprise trust questions
- an observability/evals company sees a customer gap it cannot cover

The next validation must find one named blocked workflow, not another abstract governance concern.

## Target User & Narrowest Wedge

Immediate target: Ross Stapleton-Gray at Braintrust.

Buyer hypothesis:

Braintrust-like observability/evals companies may understand the gap earlier than ordinary enterprises because they already live close to AI traces, evals, production behavior, and customer trust. Their gap is not "what happened?" It is "who gave the agent authority, what was it allowed to do, what went off-trail, who was alerted, what was blocked, and what evidence can a customer trust?"

Narrowest wedge:

Do not ask Ross whether Erebor is interesting.

Ask whether Braintrust has one of three concrete pains:

1. Customer trust gap: Braintrust customers ask for governance around agent authority and actions that observability/evals do not answer.
2. Internal rollout gap: Braintrust itself has agent workflows that cannot touch real systems without governance.
3. Buyer-path map: Ross can introduce the IT, risk, compliance, AI platform, or security owner who owns this problem.

The smallest next artifact is a one-page trust-gap packet plus a 30-minute follow-up script.

The packet should show:

- allowed trail: what the agent is supposed to do
- delegated authority: what identity/access the agent has
- off-trail action: what the agent attempts outside the mandate
- runtime decision: allow, block, hold, escalate, or stop
- evidence: who/where/why, action trace, authority trace, residual risk
- rollback/containment label: what can be undone, contained, compensated, or must be refused

## External Landscape

Layer 1: the old categories are observability, IAM, audit logs, human review, sandboxes, and runtime guardrails. These are useful but incomplete for path-dependent agent behavior.

Layer 2: current 2026 public work is converging on runtime interception, delegated authority, and path-level governance:

- AgentTrust frames unsafe file, shell, HTTP, and database tool actions as side-effect risks and proposes runtime allow/warn/block/review verdicts: https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.04785
- Runtime Governance for AI Agents argues that execution path is the core object for governing agents, because prompt and static access control do not evaluate path-dependent behavior: https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.16586
- Overlaying Governance frames delegation, permission inheritance, dynamic scope, and accountability as missing semantics in current authorization models: https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.03518
- Axios's April 2026 identity/access roundtable reinforces that enterprises are worried about intent, consent, identity, and agent access control: https://www.axios.com/2026/04/24/axios-live-expertvoices-roundtable-dc-cybersecurity-ai-agents
- ITPro's April 2026 coverage of Ping Identity research reinforces that autonomous agents create gaps in governance, visibility, accountability, delegation, and traceability: https://www.itpro.com/security/enterprises-are-adopting-agents-faster-than-they-can-secure-and-govern-them-experts-warn-its-a-disaster-waiting-to-happen

Layer 3: Erebor should not try to own all of AI security. The sharper wedge is governed-session proof for one blocked workflow: mandate, delegated authority, off-trail detection, runtime decision, reviewer evidence, and honest rollback/containment labels.

EUREKA: the category is not "observability versus governance" in the abstract. Erebor's wedge is the proof object between them: a governed session that shows what authority the agent had, what path it followed, where it left the trail, what stopped it, and what evidence a reviewer can trust.

### 30-Minute Call Script

Open:

- "Last time you said Braintrust could be a customer. I do not want to overinterpret that. What would make this a real need rather than an interesting category?"

Force the branch:

- "Is this a Braintrust internal need, a Braintrust customer need, or an intro-path to someone else?"

If internal:

- "What agent workflow do you keep away from real systems today?"
- "Who owns that decision?"
- "What would need to be true to let it run under a limited pilot?"

If customer:

- "Have customers asked Braintrust for governance around what agents are allowed to do, not only observability of what happened?"
- "What is the exact gap between evals/traces and approval?"
- "Would a governed-session proof packet help Braintrust sell, support, or partner around that gap?"

If intro:

- "Who is the buyer closest to this: IT manager, risk owner, compliance owner, AI platform lead, security architect, agent vendor founder, or customer trust lead?"
- "Can you introduce me to one person who has a real blocked workflow?"

Close:

- "Would this be worth a second working session where I bring the governed-session packet and we test it against one real workflow?"
