# Kayne Call Findings

Date: 2026-06-24

Call type: GRC / cybersecurity / AI-governance market-map interview

## Summary

The call was good because it challenged a comfortable assumption, but it did
not create the whole ICP shift by itself.

Before Kayne, we had already moved away from "DPO/legal are the buyer" toward
"DPO/legal/security/GRC are approvers, validators, or reviewers." The earlier
working model was that these reviewers could slow, enforce, or block agent
workflows unless the deploying team had better evidence and controls.

Kayne sharpened the next layer: in many organizations, GRC/legal/privacy may
not have the budget, appetite, or practical authority to block agent deployment.
They may advise, document risk, ask for contractual protections, or push
responsibility onto the vendor instead.

Kayne's signal was not "GRC is the buyer." It was closer to:

- many GRC teams are not actively trying to govern AI agents yet
- they often do not have budget for this
- they do not want to be the group that stops adoption
- they may prefer shifting responsibility through contracts
- in some companies, the implicit calculation may be that paying fines later is cheaper than fixing governance now
- real governance would ideally become governance-as-code, but the market is not there yet
- "human in the loop" can be used as a comforting story rather than a real control
- Europe may be a stronger ICP than North America because regulatory pressure is more real

This is useful, but it means the ICP needs to sharpen.

The correction is:

- Before Kayne: DPO/legal/GRC are probably not the buyer, but they may be the
  approval gate.
- After Kayne: DPO/legal/GRC may not even be a reliable gate. The stronger
  pressure may come from whoever needs the agent to be trusted enough to sell,
  buy, or deploy.

## Regulatory Capture Interpretation

Kayne's "Meta" point should be treated as a market-behavior warning, not as a
literal claim that every company captures regulators.

The pattern is closer to this:

- ship and grow before the rules are clear
- make the product behavior normal before governance catches up
- push responsibility into contracts where possible
- frame stronger rules as slowing innovation or competitiveness
- deal with enforcement later, when the company or product is already important

The Facebook/Meta history is useful because it shows how late enforcement can
arrive relative to product scale. Facebook settled FTC privacy charges in 2011.
It acquired Instagram in 2012 and WhatsApp in 2014. Cambridge Analytica became
public in 2018. The FTC penalty came in 2019. The FTC antitrust case came in
2020, years after the market structure had already formed.

That is the point for Erebor: some organizations will not wait for a clean
governance model before deploying agents. They may deploy first, normalize the
workflow, shift risk through contracts, and handle the consequences later.

So the wedge cannot depend on the idea that everyone is blocked by governance.
Some people are blocked. Some are not. Some are deliberately not trying to be.

## What This Weakens

### 1. GRC as the buyer

This call reinforces the idea that GRC/compliance teams are usually not the
primary buyer right now, at least in many organizations.

That point was already emerging before Kayne. What Kayne added is stronger:
they may not own the budget, may not want to be the blocking function, and may
prefer to move responsibility through contracts rather than buying a runtime
control layer.

Better framing:

GRC is often a constraint-setter, not the economic buyer.

### 2. "Approval is stuck" as a universal claim

Kayne pushed against the idea that deployment is always getting stuck. In some environments, organizations may simply proceed, shift risk contractually, and accept future regulatory or incident risk.

That matters because "companies cannot deploy agents without Erebor" is too strong.

Better framing:

Some organizations will deploy anyway. Erebor is most urgent where someone needs defensible approval, enterprise trust, or regulated deployment.

### 3. DPO/legal/security as guaranteed blockers

The pre-Kayne hypothesis was already moving toward "DPO/legal/security are not
buyers." But it still assumed they could often block or enforce the workflow.

Kayne weakens that assumption. In some organizations, these functions may not be
able to stop deployment directly, or may avoid becoming the team that says no.

Better framing:

DPO/legal/security/GRC can still shape the approval bar, review evidence, and
create pressure. But the buyer is more likely the team that needs the agent to
pass enterprise trust review, ship a governed pilot, or survive customer/legal
questions.

### 4. "Regulation will force everyone to care soon"

Maybe eventually, but that is not a reliable startup wedge by itself.

Kayne's point means some companies will wait, deflect, or absorb risk. They may
not buy a control layer just because the world "should" govern agents better.

Better framing:

Erebor should look for places where governance pressure already changes a real
decision: a deal, a pilot, a regulated deployment, a procurement review, or a
customer's willingness to let an agent touch real systems.

### 5. Human-in-the-loop as a sufficient control

Kayne's view that "human in the loop" can be a scheme is a strong warning. It should not be treated as a serious answer unless the human has clear authority, context, timing, and accountability.

Better framing:

The product should not merely add approval prompts. It should help define and enforce which actions require approval, what evidence the approver sees, and what happened after the decision.

## What This Strengthens

### 1. Agent vendors selling into enterprise

If enterprise buyers shift responsibility through contracts, the pressure may move to the seller.

That makes agent vendors a stronger potential ICP:

- they need to answer enterprise risk questionnaires
- they may need to win trust during security review
- they may need proof that their agent can be governed
- they may need deployment options for regulated customers
- they may use governance as a sales advantage

This path may be more urgent than selling directly to internal GRC teams.

### 2. Europe as a stronger first market

Kayne's comment that Europe may be the ICP is important.

The reason is not "Europe likes compliance." The reason is that regulatory pressure, AI governance expectations, and privacy/data-protection culture may create a stronger need for operational evidence before agent deployment.

Europe might be better for:

- privacy/legal/GRC validation
- regulated-enterprise pilots
- agent vendors selling into regulated buyers
- messaging around accountability and evidence

### 3. Governance-as-code as the product direction

Kayne's "ideal world" comment points toward a better long-term product language.

Erebor should not sound like:

"audit logs for agents"

It should move toward:

"governance-as-code for agents operating real systems"

But that phrase is still too abstract for outreach. In conversation, translate it to:

"rules outside the agent that decide what it can do, what needs approval, what gets blocked, and what evidence is kept."

### 4. Contractual risk transfer as a sales clue

If companies pass responsibility to vendors, then vendors need artifacts to survive procurement/security/legal review.

Erebor could become part of:

- security review evidence
- trust center evidence
- enterprise deployment package
- regulated-customer sales enablement
- self-hosted / customer-controlled governance layer

### 5. Enterprise trust as the economic pressure

The strongest commercial pressure may not be "the DPO wants better logs."

It may be:

- a vendor cannot close an enterprise customer without a credible control story
- an internal AI platform team cannot expand from toy pilots to real workflows
- a security/product-security owner needs a boundary outside prompts
- a European or regulated customer asks for evidence before allowing deployment

This is a better buying trigger than abstract concern about AI governance.

## Updated Hypothesis

Old hypothesis:

DPO/legal/GRC/security teams are not usually the economic buyer, but they may
block or strongly gate agent deployment when evidence and controls are missing.

Updated hypothesis:

The strongest buyer may be the team or company that needs agent deployment to
be trusted by others, especially when DPO/legal/GRC pressure shows up as review,
contract terms, procurement friction, customer trust, or regulated deployment
requirements rather than direct budget ownership.

That can be:

- an agent vendor selling to enterprises
- an AI platform team enabling internal agents
- a product/security team responsible for safe deployment
- a European or regulated organization that needs stronger evidence before rollout

GRC/legal/privacy are still important, but they may be validators and pressure sources rather than buyers.

## Revised Positioning After The Call

Do not lead with:

"Companies cannot deploy agents because legal will block them."

Lead with:

"When agents can run commands, use tools, browse, or touch SaaS access,
companies need a control boundary outside the agent. Erebor gives vendors and
enterprise teams a way to prove what the agent was allowed to do, enforce limits
during the run, and keep evidence for review."

That says:

- the product is broader than browser governance
- the boundary is outside prompts
- the value is control plus evidence
- the buyer can be either the vendor or the enterprise deployer
- legal/privacy/security can still validate the evidence

## New ICP Ranking

### Tier 1: Agent vendors selling into enterprise

Why:

- they directly lose or slow deals when trust is missing
- they need to answer buyer security/legal/GRC questions
- they may want governance as a differentiator
- responsibility may be pushed onto them by contract

Interview target:

Founders, heads of product, heads of security, solutions/security leads at agent startups.

Core question:

"Have enterprise deals slowed down because buyers asked how your agent is controlled, audited, or held accountable?"

### Tier 2: European regulated-enterprise AI/security/platform teams

Why:

- regulatory pressure may make the problem earlier and sharper
- approval evidence may matter before incidents
- privacy/security culture may be more aligned with Erebor

Interview target:

AI governance, security architecture, product security, platform, privacy engineering, regulated-industry innovation teams.

Core question:

"Are agentic workflows being limited because the organization cannot yet prove or control what the agent does?"

### Tier 3: Internal AI platform / security teams in North America

Why:

- still relevant if they are actively deploying agents
- stronger if they have executive mandate to enable agents safely
- weaker if governance is still theoretical

Interview target:

AI platform, product security, security engineering, identity/access, CISO office.

Core question:

"Who owns making agent deployments safe enough for internal use?"

### Tier 4: GRC / DPO / legal

Why:

- good for approval criteria and language
- weak as buyer unless they own AI governance tooling budget
- useful for European validation

Interview target:

People with practical review responsibility, not generic AI commentators.

Core question:

"When this comes for review, what would you need to see, and who brought it to you?"

## What To Do Next

### 1. Shift the next 20 interviews

Do not spend the next 20 calls mostly on DPO/GRC.

Recommended mix:

- 8 agent vendors selling to enterprises
- 5 European privacy/security/AI-governance people
- 4 internal AI platform/security/product-security people
- 3 GRC/DPO/legal approvers for approval-language validation

### 2. Add a vendor-specific interview script

The current scripts are too focused on approvers and internal teams. Create a dedicated script for agent vendors:

- where enterprise deals slow down
- what security/legal/procurement asks
- what evidence is hard to provide
- whether self-hosted/customer-controlled governance matters
- whether a governed runtime would help close deals
- what they would pay for versus build

### 3. Update outreach targeting

Add/search for titles like:

- Founder / CEO at agent companies
- Head of Product at agent companies
- Head of Security at AI-agent companies
- Solutions Engineering / Field CTO
- Enterprise AI product lead
- AI platform lead in regulated industries
- Security architect for AI platforms
- AI governance lead in Europe

Reduce generic:

- broad GRC consultants without deployment exposure
- DPOs with no AI-agent review experience
- AI engineers building agents as competitors rather than buyers

### 4. Test Europe deliberately

Run a small Europe-focused outreach batch.

Countries to prioritize:

- United Kingdom
- Germany
- Netherlands
- France
- Ireland
- Switzerland
- Nordics

Target regulated sectors:

- fintech
- banking
- insurance
- healthcare
- legal tech
- enterprise SaaS vendors selling into regulated buyers

The question to test:

"Is Europe actually more ready to care about runtime agent governance, or does it only produce better advisory conversations?"

### 5. Reframe the demo story

The demo should not say:

"Look, legal will block this."

Better:

"Here is what happens when an agent is allowed to act. Erebor gives the operator and reviewer a control boundary outside the agent: what it can do, what needs approval, what gets blocked, and what evidence remains."

Make the demo useful for both:

- vendor: "this helps me answer enterprise review"
- enterprise team: "this helps me pilot safely"

### 6. Treat "governance-as-code" as product direction, not first-touch copy

Use internally:

"governance-as-code for agents operating real systems"

Use externally:

"control and evidence for agents that can run commands, use tools, browse, or touch SaaS access"

### 7. Stop asking only "what evidence would approvers need?"

Add the sharper commercial questions:

- Who loses if this is not solved?
- Who pays if this becomes urgent?
- Who is contractually responsible?
- Who needs this to close a deal?
- Who needs this to ship a pilot?
- Who is embarrassed if an agent does the wrong thing?

### 8. Add a regulatory-capture test to interviews

Do not use the phrase "regulatory capture" with prospects.

Ask it in practical terms:

- Are teams deploying agents first and cleaning up governance later?
- Are customers pushing responsibility back to vendors through contracts?
- Are AI-agent vendors being asked for evidence they cannot easily provide?
- Do buyers care enough to slow or block deployment, or do they accept the risk?
- In Europe, does the review bar actually change deployment behavior?

The goal is to separate real urgency from interesting commentary.

## Questions To Ask In The Next Calls

For agent vendors:

1. Have enterprise customers asked how your agent is governed once it can take actions?
2. Where does review slow down?
3. What evidence is hardest to provide today?
4. What deployment or governance requirements do customers ask for?
5. Have you lost, delayed, or narrowed a pilot because of these concerns?
6. Would a governed runtime help you sell, deploy, or reduce custom enterprise work?
7. Are customers trying to push operational responsibility for agent behavior back onto you?
8. If you could hand a buyer a credible "agent control and evidence" package, would that change a deal?

For European/regulated enterprise teams:

1. Are agentic workflows being discussed or piloted in your organization?
2. What makes them hard to approve?
3. Who would own the operating boundary for the agent?
4. What evidence would make a limited pilot acceptable?
5. Would controls outside the agent matter, or is policy/process considered enough?
6. Who would own the budget for making this deployable?
7. Does the review bar change when the agent can run commands or touch SaaS access?
8. Is the practical concern privacy, security, accountability, reliability, or all of them?

For GRC/DPO/legal:

1. When an agent workflow comes for review, who brought it to you?
2. What would you need to see before approving a limited pilot?
3. Where do contracts push responsibility back to the vendor?
4. What evidence would make the vendor more credible?
5. Who should I talk to closer to the deployment or buying decision?

## Brutal Take

This call makes the problem more real but the buyer less obvious.

That is good. It prevents building for an imaginary GRC buyer.

The strongest next path is to test agent vendors first: companies selling AI
agents into enterprises and losing time, pilots, or deals because buyers ask
how the agent is controlled, audited, deployed, or limited.

European regulated buyers, internal AI-platform teams, security, DPO, legal,
and GRC are still useful learning targets. They explain the objections agent
vendors must overcome. They are not the primary hypothesis buyer right now.

If vendors say enterprise review is painful and they need evidence/control artifacts to close deals, Erebor has a clear wedge.

If European regulated teams say agent deployments are coming but approval requires stronger runtime evidence, Erebor has a clear wedge.

If both groups only say "interesting," then the demo is ahead of the market and
the next move is narrower: find one high-risk workflow where an agent is already
being deployed despite weak controls.

The biggest mistake after this call would be to keep interviewing only people
who can describe the governance problem but do not feel the buying pressure.
